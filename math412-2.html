Heat Equation: IBVP Recap & General Solution

We consider the IBVP with no sources and zero-temperature ends:

IBVP
\[ \begin{cases} \text{PDE:} & \displaystyle\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \quad 0 < x < L,\; t > 0,\\[6pt] \text{BC:} & u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0, \quad t > 0,\\[4pt] \text{IC:} & u(x,0) = f(x), \quad 0 \le x \le L. \end{cases} \]

Writing the initial temperature as its Fourier sine series on \([0,L]\):

\[ u(x,0) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad B_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L u(x,0)\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx. \]

Then, by the eigenvalue/eigenfunction analysis and the principle of superposition, the solution is:

General Solution
\[ u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}. \]
Where Did the \(B_n\) Formula Come From? — Orthogonality of Sines
Orthogonality of Sines (for integers \(n, m \ge 1\))
\[ \int_0^L \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\,\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx = \begin{cases} 0 & m \ne n,\\ \dfrac{L}{2} & m = n. \end{cases} \]

The value \(L/2\) is the half-interval length.

This result is the key to isolating individual \(B_n\) coefficients. It follows from Euler's identity \(e^{ix} = \cos x + i\sin x\).

Trig Identities "From Scratch" via Euler's Formula

Using \(e^{(a+b)i} = e^{ai}e^{bi}\) and Euler's identity:

\[ \cos(a+b) + i\sin(a+b) = (\cos a + i\sin a)(\cos b + i\sin b). \]

Expanding and matching real and imaginary parts:

\[ \begin{cases} \cos(a+b) = \cos a\cos b - \sin a\sin b,\\ \sin(a+b) = \sin a\cos b + \cos a\sin b. \end{cases} \]

Since cosine is even and sine is odd:

\[ \begin{cases} \cos(a-b) = \cos a\cos b + \sin a\sin b,\\ \sin(a-b) = \sin a\cos b - \cos a\sin b. \end{cases} \]

Subtracting gives the product-to-sum identity:

\[ \sin a\sin b = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(a-b) - \cos(a+b)]. \]

And from \(\cos(2x) = \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x\):

\[ \cos(2x) = 1 - 2\sin^2 x \implies \sin^2 x = \tfrac{1}{2} - \tfrac{\cos(2x)}{2}. \]
Orthogonality Proofs

Case \(n \ne m\)

Apply the product-to-sum identity:

\[ \int_0^L \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^L\!\left[\cos\frac{(n-m)\pi x}{L} - \cos\frac{(n+m)\pi x}{L}\right]dx. \]

Integrating (requires \(n \ne m\) so denominators are non-zero):

\[ = \frac{L}{2\pi}\!\left(\frac{\sin\frac{(n-m)\pi x}{L}}{n-m} - \frac{\sin\frac{(n+m)\pi x}{L}}{n+m}\right)\!\Bigg|_0^L = \frac{L}{2\pi}\!\left(\frac{\sin(n-m)\pi}{n-m} - \frac{\sin(n+m)\pi}{n+m}\right) = 0, \]

since \(\sin(k\pi) = 0\) for any integer \(k\).

Case \(n = m\)

Use the half-angle identity \(\sin^2\theta = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{\cos 2\theta}{2}\):

\[ \int_0^L \sin^2\!\frac{n\pi x}{L}\,dx = \int_0^L\!\left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{\cos\frac{2n\pi x}{L}}{2}\right)dx = \left(\frac{x}{2} - \frac{L}{4n\pi}\sin\frac{2n\pi x}{L}\right)\!\Bigg|_0^L = \frac{L}{2}. \]
Deriving the \(B_n\) Formula

Assume \(\displaystyle f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\). To find \(B_m\):

Step 1. Multiply both sides by \(\sin\dfrac{m\pi x}{L}\):

\[ f(x)\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}. \]

Step 2. Integrate from \(0\) to \(L\):

\[ \int_0^L f(x)\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx = \int_0^L\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx. \]

Step 3. By orthogonality, only the \(n = m\) term survives:

\[ \int_0^L f(x)\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx = B_m\int_0^L\sin^2\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx = B_m\cdot\frac{L}{2}. \]

Step 4. Solve for \(B_m\):

\[ B_m = \frac{\displaystyle\int_0^L f(x)\sin\dfrac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx}{\displaystyle\int_0^L\sin^2\dfrac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx} = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx. \]
The Vector Analogy

The Fourier coefficient formula is exactly the analogue of decomposing a vector in an orthogonal basis.

Finite-dimensional vectors

Orthogonal basis \(\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \mathbf{b}_3\) with \(\mathbf{b}_n\cdot\mathbf{b}_m = 0\) for \(n\ne m\), \(\|\mathbf{b}_m\|^2\) for \(n=m\).

\[ \mathbf{v} = v_1\mathbf{b}_1+v_2\mathbf{b}_2+v_3\mathbf{b}_3 \implies v_m = \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{b}_m}{\|\mathbf{b}_m\|^2}. \]
Functions on \([0,L]\)

Orthogonal "basis" \(\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\) with inner product \(\langle f,g\rangle = \int_0^L fg\,dx\).

\[ f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty B_n\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L} \implies B_m = \frac{\langle f,\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\rangle}{\|\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\|^2}. \]

\(v_1, v_2, v_3\) are the coordinates of \(\mathbf{v}\) relative to the basis \(\{\mathbf{b}_1,\mathbf{b}_2,\mathbf{b}_3\}\). Similarly, \(B_n\) are the "coordinates" of \(f\) relative to the basis of eigenfunctions.

Functions as Vectors — Inner Products on Function Spaces
Orthogonality of functions on \([0,L]\): Two functions \(f,g\) are orthogonal if \[\int_0^L f(x)\,g(x)\,dx = 0.\]

We define the inner product (also called the dot product) of functions on \([0,L]\) by:

\[ \langle f, g\rangle = \langle f(x)\,|\,g(x)\rangle := \int_0^L f(x)\,g(x)\,dx. \]

So orthogonal functions satisfy \(f \perp g\), just like orthogonal vectors satisfy \(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}=0\).

This is a scalar-valued, symmetric, bilinear map — takes two "vectors" (functions), outputs a scalar. In quantum mechanics this is called bra-ket notation: \(\langle f | g\rangle\).

In ODE/linear algebra, solution spaces were finite-dimensional. For PDEs, we have infinite-dimensional function spaces — the realm of Fourier series and functional analysis (infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces).

Worked Example: \(u(x,0) = 100\)

A physically natural experiment: boil a rod in water (so \(u = 100\,^\circ\text{C}\) everywhere at \(t=0\)), then at \(t=0\) submerge the ends in an ice bath.

IBVP
\[ \begin{cases} \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\dfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \quad 0 < x < L,\; t > 0,\\[6pt] u(0,t) = 0, \quad u(L,t) = 0, \quad t > 0,\\[4pt] u(x,0) = 100, \quad 0 < x < L. \end{cases} \]

Computing the Fourier Coefficients

\[ B_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L 100\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\,dx = -\frac{200}{n\pi}\cos\frac{n\pi x}{L}\Bigg|_0^L = \frac{200}{n\pi}(1 - \cos(n\pi)). \]

Since \(\cos(n\pi) = (-1)^n\) for integer \(n\):

\[ B_n = \begin{cases} \dfrac{400}{n\pi} & n \text{ odd}, \\ 0 & n \text{ even}. \end{cases} \]

Solution

Reindexing to sum over odd \(n\) only (writing \(n = 2m+1\), \(m = 0,1,2,\ldots\)):

Solution
\[ u(x,t) = \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{400}{(2m+1)\pi}\sin\!\left(\frac{(2m+1)\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-k[(2m+1)\pi/L]^2 t}. \]

Interactive visualization at desmos.com/calculator/aruawtcp9w. Note that the zero-temp BC means \(\phi(0) = \phi(L) = 0\) in the spatial eigenfunction, i.e. \(u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0\) for all \(t > 0\).

Insulated Ends (§2.4)

Insulated ends means no heat flux at \(x=0\) or \(x=L\). By Fourier's law \(\phi = -K_0\,\partial u/\partial x\), this means:

IBVP — Insulated Ends
\[ \begin{cases} \text{PDE:} & \displaystyle\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \quad 0 < x < L,\; t > 0,\\[6pt] \text{BC:} & \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(0,t) = \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(L,t) = 0, \quad t > 0,\\[6pt] \text{IC:} & u(x,0) = f(x). \end{cases} \]

Seeking product solutions \(u = \phi(x)G(t)\) as before gives the same time ODE \(G'=-\lambda k G\) and the spatial eigenvalue problem with Neumann (derivative) BCs:

\[ \phi''(x) = -\lambda\,\phi(x), \qquad \phi'(0) = \phi'(L) = 0. \]
Case \(\lambda > 0\)
\[ \phi(x) = c_1\cos(\sqrt{\lambda}\,x) + c_2\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}\,x), \quad \phi'(x) = \sqrt{\lambda}\bigl(-c_1\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}\,x)+c_2\cos(\sqrt{\lambda}\,x)\bigr). \]

BC \(\phi'(0)=0 \Rightarrow c_2 = 0\). BC \(\phi'(L)=0 \Rightarrow -c_1\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}L)=0\), so \(\sqrt{\lambda}L = n\pi\) and:

\[ \lambda_n = \left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^{\!2},\quad \phi_n(x) = \cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 1,2,\ldots \]
Case \(\lambda = 0\)

\(\phi'' = 0 \Rightarrow \phi(x) = c_1 + c_2 x\), so \(\phi'(x)=c_2\). BC forces \(c_2=0\), leaving \(\phi(x) = c_1 =\) constant. \(\lambda=0\) IS an eigenvalue here (unlike zero-temp ends).

For \(\lambda=0\): \(G'=0 \Rightarrow G = \text{const}\), giving the product solution \(\phi(x)G(t) = A_0\) (a constant).

Case \(\lambda < 0\)

Similar analysis shows the BC force \(c_1=c_2=0\). No nontrivial solutions.

General Solution — Insulated Ends

Solution
\[ u(x,t) = A_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}, \] where the Fourier cosine coefficients are: \[ A_0 = \frac{1}{L}\int_0^L u(x,0)\,dx, \qquad A_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L u(x,0)\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx \quad (n \ge 1). \]

This is called the Fourier cosine series of \(f(x)\) on \([0,L]\).

Orthogonality of Cosines on \([0,L]\)

\[ \int_0^L \cos\frac{n\pi x}{L}\cos\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx = \begin{cases} 0 & m \ne n,\\ \dfrac{L}{2} & m = n \ne 0,\\ L & m = n = 0. \end{cases} \]

The \(m=n=0\) case gives \(L\) (the full interval length) because \(\cos(0) = 1\) and \(\int_0^L 1\,dx = L\).

Physical interpretation: As \(t\to\infty\), all terms with \(n\ge 1\) decay to zero, leaving \(u\to A_0 = \frac{1}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\,dx\) — the average initial temperature. This makes physical sense: with insulated ends, no heat can escape, so the rod equilibrates at its average initial temperature.
No Ends — Periodic Boundary Conditions (Ring)

Imagine bending the rod into a circle so the two ends are joined with "perfect thermal contact." Taking the rod to have length \(2L\) with \(-L \le x \le L\):

  • Temperature agrees at the join: \(u(-L,t) = u(L,t)\)
  • Heat flux agrees at the join: \(\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(-L,t) = \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(L,t)\)

These are called periodic boundary conditions.

IBVP — Periodic BCs
\[ \begin{cases} \text{PDE:} & \displaystyle\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \quad -L < x < L,\; t > 0,\\[6pt] \text{BC:} & u(-L,t) = u(L,t), \quad \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(-L,t) = \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(L,t), \quad t > 0,\\[6pt] \text{IC:} & u(x,0) = f(x), \quad -L \le x \le L. \end{cases} \]

Eigenvalue Problem with Periodic BCs (\(\lambda > 0\))

The general solution \(\phi(x) = c_1\cos(\sqrt{\lambda}\,x)+c_2\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}\,x)\) must satisfy \(\phi(-L)=\phi(L)\) and \(\phi'(-L)=\phi'(L)\). Using cosine-even, sine-odd:

\[ \begin{cases} -c_2\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}L) = +c_2\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}L),\\ \sqrt{\lambda}\,c_1\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}L) = -\sqrt{\lambda}\,(-c_1\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}L)). \end{cases} \]

Both reduce to \(c_1\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}L)=0\) and \(c_2\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}L)=0\). As long as \(\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}L)=0\), i.e. \(\lambda = (n\pi/L)^2\), there are no restrictions on \(c_1\) or \(c_2\). So each eigenvalue has two linearly independent eigenfunctions:

\[ \phi(x) = \cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \quad\text{and}\quad \phi(x) = \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 1, 2, \ldots \]

The \(\lambda=0\) case (\(\phi=\text{const}\)) and the \(\lambda<0\) case (no solutions) work as before.

General Solution — Periodic BCs

Solution (Full Fourier Series)
\[ u(x,t) = a_0 + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}, \] where the full Fourier series coefficients of \(f(x)\) on \([-L,L]\) are: \[ a_0 = \frac{1}{2L}\int_{-L}^{L}f(x)\,dx, \quad a_m = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L}f(x)\cos\!\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right)dx, \quad b_m = \frac{1}{L}\int_{-L}^{L}f(x)\sin\!\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right)dx. \]

Orthogonality on \([-L,L]\)

\[ \int_{-L}^{L}\cos\frac{n\pi x}{L}\cos\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx = \begin{cases} 0 & m\ne n,\\ L & m=n\ne 0,\\ 2L & m=n=0, \end{cases} \] \[ \int_{-L}^{L}\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\,dx = \begin{cases} 0 & m\ne n,\\ L & m=n\ne 0, \end{cases} \] \[ \int_{-L}^{L}\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\sin\!\left(\frac{m\pi x}{L}\right)dx = 0 \quad \text{(all } m,n\text{).} \]

§1.5 — Deriving the Heat Equation in Multiple Space Dimensions

Consider an arbitrary solid region \(R \subset \mathbb{R}^3\). The same conservation idea applies:

\[ \underbrace{\text{rate of change of heat in }R}_{\displaystyle\iiint_R c\rho\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\,dV} = \underbrace{\text{heat flow in across boundary}}_{\text{surface integral}} + \underbrace{\text{heat generated inside}}_{\displaystyle\iiint_R Q\,dV}. \]

Here \(u(x,y,z,t)\) is temperature; \(c\) and \(\rho\) (specific heat and mass density) may depend on position but not time; \(Q(x,y,z,t)\) is the source term.

The total heat and its rate of change:

\[ \text{total heat in }R = \iiint_R c\rho u\,dV,\qquad \text{rate of change} = \iiint_R c\rho\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\,dV. \]

The heat generated inside per unit time:

\[ \iiint_R Q\,dV. \]
Flux is a Vector — The Divergence Theorem

In 1D, heat flux \(\phi(x,t)\) was a scalar (positive = rightward). In 3D, we use a vector:

\[ \vec{\phi}(x,y,z,t) \quad \left[\frac{\text{J}}{\text{s}\cdot\text{m}^2}\right] \]

representing the flow of heat energy per unit time per unit surface area.

The rate at which heat flows into region \(R\) across its closed boundary surface \(S\) (with outward unit normal \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\)) is:

\[ \text{heat energy flowing in across } S = -{\huge\unicode{x222F}}_S \vec{\phi}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS. \]

(The minus sign because \(\hat{\mathbf{n}}\) points outward, so \(\vec{\phi}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}} > 0\) means heat is leaving.)

Divergence Theorem (Gauss's Theorem)
\[ -{\huge\unicode{x222F}}_S \vec{\phi}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS = -\iiint_R \nabla\cdot\vec{\phi}\,dV, \] where \(S\) is the closed outward-oriented boundary surface of the solid region \(R\).
How the divergence theorem relates to FTC:
  • 1D: \(\int_a^b F'(x)\,dx = F(b)-F(a)\) — FTC (derivative is "anti-derivative")
  • 3D: \(\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n}\,dS = \iiint_Q \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}\,dV\) — divergence theorem (divergence is "anti-divergence")
  • General: Stokes' theorem \(\int_{\partial\Omega}\omega = \int_\Omega d\omega\)

Putting everything together:

\[ \iiint_R c\rho\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\,dV = -\iiint_R \nabla\cdot\vec{\phi}\,dV + \iiint_R Q\,dV. \]

Since this holds for every region \(R\), and the integrand is continuous, we get the PDE:

\[ c\rho\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot\vec{\phi} + Q. \]
Vector Derivative Review — \(\nabla\), Gradient, Divergence, Curl

The del operator:

\[ \nabla = \left\langle\frac{\partial}{\partial x},\frac{\partial}{\partial y},\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right\rangle = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\mathbf{k}. \]

Multi-D Heat PDE & the Laplacian

Fourier's Law in Space

Heat flows in the direction of greatest decrease of temperature (opposite to \(\nabla u\)):

\[ \vec{\phi} = -K_0\,\nabla u. \]

(\(\nabla u\) points in the direction of greatest increase of \(u\); so \(-K_0\nabla u\) points toward cooler regions.)

The Multi-D Heat Equation (General)

\[ c\rho\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \nabla\cdot(K_0\,\nabla u) + Q. \]

The Laplacian

For constant thermal properties, define \(k := K_0/(c\rho)\) and the Laplacian:

\[ \Delta u = \nabla^2 u := \nabla\cdot(\nabla u) = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial z^2}. \]
Multi-D Heat Equation
With sources: \(\displaystyle\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\nabla^2 u + \frac{Q}{\rho c}\).

Without sources: \[\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\nabla^2 u.\] In 1D this reduces to \(\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\dfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\) ✓
Steady States — Laplace's & Poisson's Equations

Laplace's Equation

A steady-state solution is independent of time: \(u = u(x,y,z)\). Without sources, \(\partial u/\partial t = 0\) forces:

Laplace's Equation (Potential Equation)
\[\nabla^2 u = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial y^2}+\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial z^2} = 0.\]

Functions satisfying Laplace's equation are called harmonic functions. Laplace's equation is the prototypical elliptic PDE; the heat equation is the prototypical parabolic PDE.

Physical connections: Where sources are zero, gravitational potential, electrostatic potential, and fluid velocity potential all satisfy Laplace's equation!

Poisson's Equation

Steady states with sources (constant \(K_0\)):

\[ \nabla^2 u(x,y,z) = -\frac{Q(x,y,z)}{K_0}. \]

This "inhomogeneous Laplace equation" is called Poisson's equation.

Newtonian Gravity

If \(u\) is the gravitational potential, the gravitational force field is \(\mathbf{F} = -\nabla u\) and the potential satisfies:

\[ -\nabla^2 u \propto \rho, \]

where \(\rho\) is the mass density distribution.

Connection to Gauss's Law (Physics 212)

Maxwell's equations give \(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} \propto \rho\) (charge density). By the divergence theorem:

\[ {\huge\unicode{x222F}}_S \mathbf{E}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS = c\iiint_R \rho\,dV = c\cdot(\text{total charge in }R). \]

As long as \(\rho \equiv 0\) in the region between two nested closed surfaces \(S_1\) (inner) and \(S_2\) (outer):

\[ {\huge\unicode{x222F}}_{S_{1,\text{in}}} \mathbf{E}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS + {\huge\unicode{x222F}}_{S_{2,\text{out}}} \mathbf{E}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}\,dS = \iiint_{\text{between}} \nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}\,dV = 0. \]

This is why you can choose any Gaussian test surface surrounding all the charges — the flux integral always gives the same answer. The divergence theorem is the rigorous justification of Gauss's law in integral form.
