Intro to PDEs and Ch 1: Heat Equation

Quick DE and ODE Review

A differential equation is an equation involving an unknown function and its derivative(s).
For ordinary differential equations, this unknown dependent variable is a function of a single independent variable. Most often, we think of this input as time t, so that the ordinary differential equation describes a dynamical system.

Example of ODEs you saw in Math 250/1

  • Simple exponential growth/decay: x′ = ax

  • Separable first order: $$\frac{dx}{dt} = f(t)g(y)$$

  • First order linear: $$\frac{dx}{dt} + P(t)x = Q(t)$$

  • Driven mass-spring-dashpot mx″ + cx′ + kx = F(t)

  • Constant coefficient homogeneous n × n first order system: x′ = Ax

What is a PDE?

A partial differential equation is an equation involving an unknown function and its partial derivatives — so we are looking for a function of several variables when solving a PDE.
We’ll have cases where the dependence is on space and time, or on multiple spatial variables, or both, or ...
We’ll briefly mention certain examples and how our solution techniques can be extended to 3 or more independent variables, but for most of Math 412 we’ll focus on the case of two independent variables.

Examples of PDEs we’ll see in 412

  • Simple transport: ut + cux = 0

  • Wave equation: utt = c2uxx

  • Heat/diffusion equation: ut = kuxx

  • Laplace’s equation: uxx + uyy = 0 — describes steady-state/equilibrium solutions of 2d heat equation

  • Burgers’ equation: ut + uux = 0 — a “toy model" for gas dynamics, simplest example of a “nonlinear wave equation"

Similar to how we spent time in Math 250/1 studying constant coefficient homogeneous ODE, the first four examples on the previous slide are prototypical examples of constant coefficient homogeneous PDE.

Deriving the heat equation

Goal: write equations governing the transfer of thermal energy — start with a simple example of conduction in one-dimensional solid rod.
Conduction — transfer of heat due to collisions of neighboring molecules — kinetic energy of vibration of one molecule is transferred to its nearest neighbor; occurs even if molecules themselves aren’t moving much.
Convection — moving molecules carry thermal energy from one place to another
We focus on the case of solids, where conduction is the only means of heat transfer. However, conduction is still the primary method of heat transfer in liquids/gases if the fluid velocity is sufficiently small.

Defining physical quantities

Consider a rod with constant cross-sectional area A (perpendicular to the x-direction) occupying 0 ≤ x ≤ L.
Assume all thermal quantities are constant across a section, so that $$\begin{aligned} e(x,t) &:= \mbox{thermal energy density per unit volume},\\ \\ \phi(x,t) &:= \mbox{heat flux: thermal energy \emph{per unit time}} \\ & \mbox{ flowing to the right \emph{per unit surface area}}\\ \\ Q(x,t) &:= \mbox{sources: thermal energy per unit volume generated}\\ &\mbox{ per unit time, eg chemical reactions, electrical heating} \end{aligned}$$

Conservation of heat energy

Consider an arbitrary part of the rod occupying a ≤ x ≤ b. The change in the total heat energy contained in this portion must be due to either heat flowing across boundaries or being generated inside: $$\begin{aligned} \dfrac{d}{dt} \int_a^b A e(x, t) \, dx &= A \phi(a, t) - A \phi(b, t) + \int_a^b A Q(x,t) \, dx, \\ \\ \\ \\ \dfrac{d}{dt} \int_a^b e(x, t) \, dx &= \phi(a, t) - \phi(b, t) + \int_a^b Q(x,t) \, dx \end{aligned}$$

Some calculus...

e(x, t) differentiable and a, b constant means $$\dfrac{d}{dt} \int_a^b e(x,t) \, dx = \int_a^b \frac{\partial e}{\partial t} \, dx,$$ and Fundamental Theorem of Calculus yields $$\phi(a,t) - \phi(b,t) = -\int_a^b \dfrac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} \, dx.$$ Therefore, we can rearrange to what we can call the integral conservation law $$\int_a^b \left( \frac{\partial e}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} - Q \right) dx = 0.$$

Key idea: Assuming everything is continuous, the only way this integral could be zero for arbitrary a and b is if the integrand itself is identically zero.
Therefore, we can get the differential equation $$\frac{\partial e}{\partial t} = - \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + Q.$$

Temperature, specific heat

We define $$\begin{aligned} u(x,t) &= \mbox{temperature},\\ c(x) &= \mbox{specific heat --- heat energy needed }\\ & \mbox{per unit mass to raise temperature one unit},\\ \rho(x) &= \mbox{mass density --- mass per unit volume} \end{aligned}$$ so that e(x, t) = c(x)ρ(x)u(x, t), leading to $$c(x) \rho(x) \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = - \frac{\partial \phi}{\partial x} + Q.$$

More physics — Fourier’s law

Up to now, essentially just definitions and calculus — the only physics so far was conservation of heat energy.
To proceed further, we’ll need to use another “law of nature" — the rule for relating the flux to the temperature distribution.

  • If the temperature is constant in a region, no heat energy flows.

  • If there are temperature differences, the heat energy flows from the hotter region to the colder region.

  • The greater the temperature differences (for the same material), the greater the flow of heat energy.

  • The flow of heat energy will vary for different materials, even with the same temperature differences.

More physics — Fourier’s law

Fourier’s Law of Heat Conduction: (1768-1830): $$\phi = - K_0 \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x},$$ where K0 is the thermal conductivity — constant of proportionality measuring the ability of the material to conduct heat.
Actually, thermal conductivity does slightly depend on temperature (technically so does specific heat), but we’ll assume it only depends on the material so that K0 = K0(x) or K0 = const for a uniform rod.

Putting it all together, $$c(x) \rho(x) \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x} \left( K_0(x) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \right) + Q(x,t).$$ Assuming a uniform rod (so that specific heat, mass density, thermal conductivity are constants) and zero source leads to $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k \frac{\partial ^2 u}{\partial x^2},$$ where $k = \frac{K_0}{c \rho}$ is the thermal diffusivity. The above equation is what we refer to as “the heat equation" or “the diffusion equation".

Initial Conditions

Recall that the DE $\frac{dx}{dt}=ax$ has infinitely many solutions — x = Ceat for any constant C.
Only when we consider an initial value problem $$\left\{ \begin{array}{rl} \frac{dx}{dt} &= ax \\ \\ x(0) &= x_0 \end{array} \right.$$ do we obtain a unique solution x = x0eat.
The modeling and mathematics are deeply connected — for modeling we are interested in figuring out what information is needed to predict the future; being able to trust the prediction requires that only one prediction is made — so uniqueness is a very important consideration.

Since we expect infinitely many solutions to the heat equation $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2},$$ if we want to use this equation to make reliable modeling predictions, we’ll need to determine what other conditions (in addition to the PDE) will allow us to uniquely predict the future.
It turns out we’ll need both initial and boundary conditions.

Recall we’re considering the time AND spatial dependence of temperature given by u(x, t), where the one-dimensional rod occupies 0 ≤ x ≤ L.
The initial condition in this case is the entire initial temperature distribution u(x, 0) = f(x). It makes intuitive sense that the only way we’d be able to predict the temperature of all points of the rod in the future is if we know the temperature of all points at a given time (which we always pick t = 0 for convenience).

Unfortunately, there are infinitely many possible solutions u(x, t) satisfying $$\left\{ \begin{array}{rl} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \\ \\ u(x,0) &= f(x) \end{array} \right. .$$ In order to have a problem that is useful for modeling predictions, we’ll need to prescribe boundary conditions — one for the x = 0 boundary, and one for the x = L boundary.

We need to describe what happens on each end. For x = 0, we could have

  • u(0, t) = uB(t) — prescribed temperature, “known" fluid bath temperature uB(t).

  • $-K_0(0) \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(0,t) = \phi(t)$ — prescribed heat flux; ϕ(t) ≡ 0 is “perfectly insulated"

  • could also set up Newton’s law of cooling for cases where convection in fluid bath is important

We’ll see that the following initial-boundary value problem $$\left\{ \begin{array}{rl} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \\ u(x,0) &= f(x) \\ u(0,t) &= T_1(t) \\ u(L,t) &= T_2(t) \end{array} \right.$$ does have unique solutions! That is, knowing the temperature of the entire rod at t = 0, and the temperature of each of the two ends for t > 0, then we are able to find the unique temperature distribution u(x, t) for 0 ≤ x ≤ L, t > 0.
Goal: Solve for the temperature u(x, t) on the rod for all t > 0.

We’ll move ahead to 2.1 for now to solve the 1d heat equation; if you’re curious about the 2d or 3d derivation now you can take a look at 1.5 in the textbook.
It follows all the same steps, but it becomes necessary to think about flux as a vector quantity. The key FTC step “becomes" “2-d divergence theorem"/“Green’s theorem for flux" or the divergence theorem.

Ch 2: Method of Separation of Variables

Unfortunate nomenclature clash between “ODE 101" and “PDE 101": separable ODEs vs. separation of variables for PDEs
Separable ODEs:

  • property of the equation — of the form $\frac{dy}{dt} = f(y)g(t)$

  • quick and easy to see via chain rule / integration by substitution that following the prescribed technique leads to solutions; existence and uniqueness for ODEs shows “we’re done"

Ch 2: Method of Separation of Variables

Separation of variables for PDEs:

  • suitable for IVBPs when the PDE and BC are linear and homogeneous

  • corresponds to an ansatz/educated guess u(x, t) = ϕ(x)G(t) regarding the form of the solution — completely unclear why this form is chosen, first done by Daniel Bernoulli in 1700s. We’ll continue to go with what’s been working...

  • Specialized PDE theories can establish existence and uniqueness — heat, wave, Laplace’s equation are all completely different!

Linear operators

A linear operator L satisfies the linearity property: L(c1u1 + c2u2) = c1L(u1) + c2L(u2)     for all u1, u2, c1, c2. We have the heat operator $$L(u) := \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} - k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}.$$

Verifying the heat operator is linear

$$\begin{aligned} L(c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2) &= \frac{\partial}{\partial t} (c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2) - k \frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2} (c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2) \\ &= c_1 \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial t} + c_2 \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial t} - k c_1 \frac{\partial^2 u_1}{\partial x^2} - k c_2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \\ &= c_1 \left( \frac{\partial u_1}{\partial t} - k \frac{\partial^2 u_1}{\partial x^2} \right) + c_2 \left( \frac{\partial u_2}{\partial t} - k \frac{\partial^2 u_2}{\partial x^2} \right) \\ &= c_1 L(u_1) + c_2 L(u_2). \end{aligned}$$

Linear equations

A linear equation for the unknown function u is of the form L(u) = f, where L is a linear operator and f is known. The heat equation takes the form $$L(u) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} - k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} = f(x,t),$$ where $f(x,t) = \frac{Q(x,t)}{c \rho}$ is the source.
If f = 0, the equation L(u) = 0 is a linear homogeneous equation.

Since L(0) = 0 for all linear operators, the trivial solution u ≡ 0 solves every linear homogeneous equation.
If f ≠ 0 then the equation is nonhomogeneous.
The symbol “≡" is read as “identically equals" — it emphasizes that u(x, t) = 0 for all x, t under consideration, instead of just equalling zero for certain x, t. You can also read u ≡ 0 as u is defined to be the (constant) zero function of x and t.

Principle of Superposition

If u1 and u2 satisfy a linear homogeneous equation, then an arbitrary linear combination of them c1u1 + c2u2 also satisfies the equation.
In other words, the solution space of a linear homogeneous equation forms a vector space.

In Math 220 and 250, your linear maps / linear ordinary differential equations had finite dimensional vector spaces as their solution spaces — linear algebra/matrix techniques (finite dimensional Hilbert spaces) are relevant.
In this class, our PDE will have infinite dimensional vector spaces as their solution spaces. This is the purview of Fourier series (function spaces, functional analysis, infinite dimensional Hilbert spaces, ...)

Homogeneous boundary conditions

The cases of
prescribed temperature: u(⋅, t) = f(t),
prescribed heat flux: $\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(\cdot, t) = f(t)$,
or Newtown’s law of cooling (prescribed temp bath with convection): $-K_0 \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(\cdot, t) = h[u(\cdot, t)-f(t)]$
will not be homogeneous in general.
Two cases that are homogeneous: zero temperature ends and insulated ends.

No sources, zero temperature ends

We consider $$\left\{ \begin{array}{crl} \mbox{PDE: } & \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, & 0 < x < L, t > 0 \\ \mbox{BC: } & u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0, & t > 0 \\ \mbox{IC: } & u(x,0) = f(x), & 0 \leq x \leq L \end{array} \right. .$$

Separation of variables ansatz

We seek a product solution to the PDE and BC of the form u(x, t) = ϕ(x)G(t). We ignore the IC for now — later the principle of superposition and properties of Fourier series will allow us to use an appropriate infinite sum of such product solutions to obtain a solution that also satisfies the IC.
Math 250 analogue: Used Euler’s ansatz y(t) = ert for higher order constant coefficient equations, and eigenvalue/vector ansatz x(t) = eλtv for first order constant coefficient systems. At that step, you were ignoring IC. Linearity and properties of linear independence/Wronskians/eigenbases allowed you to use an appropriate finite sum to obtain a solution that also satisfied the IC.

Separation of variables key steps

Our ansatz yields $$\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= \phi(x) G'(t), \\ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} &= \phi''(x) G(t). \end{aligned}$$ Substitute into the heat equation to yield $$\phi (x) G'(t) = k \phi''(x) G(t) \implies \frac{1}{kG(t)} G'(t) = \frac{1}{\phi(x)} \phi''(x).$$ Since x and t are independent variables, the only way the above quantities can be equal for arbitrary x and t is if they’re both constant! We then introduce $$\frac{1}{kG(t)} G'(t) = \frac{1}{\phi(x)} \phi''(x) = -\lambda,$$ where λ is the separation constant.

Separation of variables key steps

The upshot of the separation of variables ansatz is that the constant coefficient homogeneous PDE is reduced to two coupled constant coefficient homogeneous ODEs $$\begin{aligned} \phi''(x) &= - \lambda \phi(x), \\ G'(t) &= - \lambda k G(t). \end{aligned}$$ The BC become ϕ(0)G(t) = ϕ(L)G(t) = 0 for all t > 0. One possibility is G(t) ≡ 0, leading to the trivial solution u(x, t) ≡ 0.
More interesting would be nontrivial solutions which must have ϕ(0) = ϕ(L) = 0.

Time-dependent ODE

Looking at G′(t) = −λkG(t) as a constant coefficient homogeneous ODE, G′ + λkG = 0 has characteristic polynomial r + λk = 0 and so G(t) = ce−λkt. If λ < 0, this would cause exponentially growing temperatures as t → ∞ — not consistent with what we expect with our zero temperature ends. We’ll see indeed the space-dependent ODE and BC require λ ≥ 0, which is why we introduced the explicit minus sign earlier.

Space-dependent ODE and BC: Eigenvalue problem

We have $$\begin{aligned} \phi''(x) &= - \lambda \phi(x), \\ \phi(0) &= 0, \\ \phi(L) &= 0. \end{aligned}$$ An eigenvalue-eigenfunction pair is a value for λ and a nontrivial solution ϕ(x) to the above ODE and BC. The eigenvalue problem is to determine all the possible eigenvalue-eigenfunction pairs.

The characteristic polynomial of this constant coefficient ODE is r2 + λ = 0, leading to three qualitatively different cases: λ > 0, λ = 0, λ < 0.

Space-dependent ODE and BC: λ > 0

We have $$\phi(x) = c_1 \cos(\sqrt{\lambda} x) + c_2 \sin(\sqrt{\lambda} x).$$ The left boundary yields c1 = 0, and the right boundary yields $$0 = c_2 \sin(\sqrt{\lambda} L).$$ The c2 = 0 possibility yields the trivial solution u(x, t) ≡ 0, and so for nontrivial solutions we must have $$\sqrt{\lambda} L = n\pi \mbox{ for some positive integer } n,$$ and so the separation constant λ satisfies $$\lambda = \left( \frac{n \pi}{L} \right)^2$$ and then $$\phi(x) = c \sin\left( \frac{n\pi x}{L} \right).$$

Space-dependent ODE and BC: λ = 0

For this case, r = 0 is a double root and so ϕ(x) = c1e0x + c2xe0x = c1 + c2x, but then the BC force c1 = c2 = 0, so no nontrivial solutions exist for λ = 0.
It seems λ = 0 is NOT an eigenvalue for this particular problem — but this is not always the case! This needs to be checked individually.

Space-dependent ODE and BC: λ < 0

Here we have real roots and so $$\phi(x) = c_1 e^{\sqrt{-\lambda} x} + c_2 e^{-\sqrt{-\lambda}x}.$$ The boundary conditions become $$\begin{aligned} c_1 + c_2 &= 0,\\ c_1 e^{L \sqrt{-\lambda}} + c_2 e^{-L\sqrt{-\lambda}} &=0 \implies c_1 + c_2 e^{-2L\sqrt{-\lambda}} = 0. \end{aligned}$$ This means $$c_2 (1-e^{-2L\sqrt{-\lambda}}) = 0 \implies c_2 = c_1 = 0,$$ and so no nontrivial solutions can exist for λ < 0.

Final form of product solutions for zero-temperature ends

The only possibility was $\lambda = \frac{n \pi}{L}$ for n > 0 integer, and we have $$u(x,t) = \phi(x) G(t) = B \sin \left( \frac{n \pi x}{L} \right) e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}.$$

Some IC are very easy to satisfy

Solve the IBVP $$\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \\ u(0,t) &= 0, \\ u(L,t) &= 0, \\ u(x,0) &= 4 \sin\left( \dfrac{3\pi x}{L} \right). \end{aligned}$$

Some IC are very easy to satisfy

Solve the IBVP $$\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \\ u(0,t) &= 0, \\ u(L,t) &= 0, \\ u(x,0) &= 7 \sin\left( \dfrac{8\pi x}{L} \right). \end{aligned}$$

Superposition

Solve the IBVP $$\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \\ u(0,t) &= 0, \\ u(L,t) &= 0, \\ u(x,0) &= 4 \sin\left( \dfrac{3\pi x}{L} \right) + 7 \sin\left( \dfrac{8\pi x}{L} \right). \end{aligned}$$

Superposition

Solve the IBVP $$\begin{aligned} \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} &= k \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \\ u(0,t) &= 0, \\ u(L,t) &= 0, \\ u(x,0) &= \sum_{n=1}^M B_n \sin\left( \dfrac{n \pi x}{L} \right) . \end{aligned}$$

Fourier Series Theory

What if the initial temperature u(x, 0) isn’t such a finite combination of sines?

  1. With some restrictions, any u(x, 0) can be approximated by such a finite linear combination of sines by choosing $$B_n = \frac 2 L \int_0^L u(x,0) \sin\left( \frac{n \pi x}{L} \right)\, dx \mbox{ for integer } n \geq 1.$$

  2. In general this may not be accurate for small M, but becomes better and better as M is increased.

  3. As M increases, NOT ONLY does the above choice for Bn’s provide the most accurate approximation of u(x, 0) as a linear combination of eigenfunctions of the PDE/BC, but it converges to u(x, 0) as M → ∞ (with some more reasonable restrictions on u(x, 0)).

That is, for “any" initial temperature distribution u(x, 0) = f(x) we can write it as its Fourier sine series $$f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty B_n \sin\left( \frac{n \pi x}{L} \right)$$ where $$B_n = \frac 2 L \int_0^L f(x) \sin\left( \frac{n \pi x}{L} \right)\, dx \mbox{ for integer } n \geq 1.$$ Then, the PDE/BC eigenvalue/eigenfunction analysis and the principle of superposition means $$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty B_n \sin\left( \frac{n \pi x}{L} \right) e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}.$$ solves the IBVP consisting of the heat equation, the BC u(0, t) = u(L, t) = 0, and the IC u(x, 0) = f(x).
