(i)¶

For $n=2$, we have $$e^{\mathbf{A}t} = p_1(t)\mathbf{M}_0 + p_2(t) \mathbf{M}_1$$ where $$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{M}_0 &= \mathbf{I}, \\ \\ \mathbf{M}_1 &= \prod_{k=1}^1 (\mathbf{A}-\lambda_k \mathbf{I}) = (\mathbf{A}-\lambda_1\mathbf{I}),\\ \\ p_1' &= \lambda_1 p_1, \quad \quad \quad p_1(0)=1, \\ p_2' &= p_1 + \lambda_2 p_2, \quad p_2(0) = 0. \end{align*} $$

The eigenvalues are given by (using the trace-determinant shortcut from earlier)

$$ 0 = \lambda^2 - (1 + -1) \lambda + [(1)(-1) - 2(-5)] = \lambda^2 + 9,$$

and so $\lambda = \pm 3i.$ We can label $\lambda_1 = 3i, \lambda_2 = -3i.$

We have

$$\mathbf{M}_1 = \mathbf{A} - 3i \mathbf{I} = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 - 3i & 2 \\ -5 & -1-3i \end{array} \right].$$

Next,

$$ p_1' = 3i p_1, \quad p_1(0) = 1 \implies p_1(t) = e^{3i t}.$$

Then,

$$ p_2' = p_1 -3i p_2 \implies p_2' + 3i p_2 = e^{3i t}.$$

We can use the integrating factor $e^{\int 3i \, dt} = e^{3it},$ leading to

$$p_2' e^{3it} + 3i e^{3it} p_2 = e^{6it} \implies \frac{d}{dt}\left( p_2 e^{3i t} \right) = e^{6it} \implies p_2 e^{3it} = \frac{1}{6i} e^{6i t} + C.$$

The initial condition $p_2(0) = 0$ means $C = -\dfrac{1}{6i}$ and so

$$p_2 e^{3i t} = \frac{1}{6i}e^{6it} - \frac{1}{6i} \implies p_2 = \frac{1}{6i}e^{3it} - \frac{1}{6i}e^{-3it} = \frac 1 3 \sin(3t),$$

using $\sin(kt) = \dfrac{e^{ikt}-e^{-ikt}}{2i}.$

Putting it together,

$$ \begin{align*} e^{\mathbf{A}t} &= p_1(t) \mathbf{M}_0 + p_2(t) \mathbf{M}_1 \\ \\ &= e^{3i t} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] + \frac 1 3 \sin(3t) \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1-3i & 2 \\ -5 & -1-3i \end{array} \right] \\ \\ &= [\cos(3t) + i \sin(3t)] \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] + \frac 1 3 \sin(3t) \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1-3i & 2 \\ -5 & -1-3i \end{array} \right] \\ \\ &= \left[ \begin{array}{cc} \cos(3t) + \frac 1 3 \sin(3t) & \frac{2}{3} \sin(3t) \\ -\frac{5}{3} \sin(3t) & \cos(3t) - \frac 1 3 \sin(3t) \end{array} \right]. \end{align*} $$

2.34¶

(i)¶

Recall all solutions to $$\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x}$$ are given by $$\mathbf{x} = e^{\mathbf{A}t}\mathbf{c},$$ where $\mathbf{c}$ is any constant vector. We have eigenvalues given by $$ (1-\lambda)^2 - 1 = 0,$$ or $1-\lambda = \pm 1,$ and so $\lambda = 0, 2$ are our eigenvalues. We take $\lambda_1 = 0$ so that $$\mathbf{M}_1 = \mathbf{A} - \lambda_1 \mathbf{I} = \mathbf{A} = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{array} \right].$$ Then we have $$ p_1' = 0, \qquad p_1(0) = 1$$ gives $$p_1(t) = 1.$$ Then the ODE for $p_2$ becomes $$p_2' - 2 p_2 = 1, \qquad p_2(0) = 0.$$ The complementary solution is $p_{2,c} = c_1e^{2t}$, and our particular solution form for undetermined coefficients is $p_{2,p} = a$. Subbing into the inhomogeneous ODE yields $$-2a = 1,$$ and so $a = - \frac 1 2$. Then $$p_2 = p_{2,c} + p_{2,p} = c_1e^{2t} - \frac 1 2,$$ and the initial condition $p_2(0) = 0$ means $c_1 = \frac 1 2$. Therefore, $$p_2(t) = \frac 1 2 e^{2t} - \frac 1 2.$$ Putting it together, $$e^{\mathbf{A}t} = p_1(t) \mathbf{M}_0 + p_2(t) \mathbf{M}_1 = 1 \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] + \left( \frac 1 2 e^{2t} - \frac 1 2 \right) \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} \frac 1 2 e^{2t} + \frac 1 2 & \frac 1 2 e^{2t} - \frac 1 2 \\ \frac 1 2 e^{2t} - \frac 1 2 & \frac 1 2 e^{2t} + \frac 1 2 \end{array} \right].$$ Then, the general solution is given by $$ \mathbf{x}(t) = e^{\mathbf{A}t} \mathbf{c} = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} \frac 1 2 e^{2t} + \frac 1 2 & \frac 1 2 e^{2t} - \frac 1 2 \\ \frac 1 2 e^{2t} - \frac 1 2 & \frac 1 2 e^{2t} + \frac 1 2 \end{array} \right] \left[ \begin{array}{c} c_1 \\ c_2 \end{array} \right] = c_1 \left[ \begin{array}{c} \frac 1 2 e^{2t} + \frac 1 2 \\ \frac 1 2 e^{2t} - \frac 1 2 \end{array} \right] + c_2 \left[ \begin{array}{c} \frac 1 2 e^{2t} - \frac 1 2 \\ \frac 1 2 e^{2t} + \frac 1 2 \end{array} \right].$$ You might find it useful to remember that if $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2$ are the columns of a square $2 \times 2$ matrix that $$\left[ \mathbf{v}_1 ~ \mathbf{v}_2 \right] \mathbf{c} = c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{v}_2.$$

$$ \mathbf{x}_1 = \frac 1 2 \mathbf{y}_1 - \frac 1 2 \mathbf{y}_2, \qquad \mathbf{x}_2 = \frac 1 2 \mathbf{y}_1 + \frac 1 2 \mathbf{y}_2,$$

the span (that is, set of all linear combinations) of $\mathbf{x}_1$ and $\mathbf{x}_2$ is the same as the span of $\mathbf{y}_1$ and $\mathbf{y}_2$.

(ii)¶

The eigenvalues are given by $$ \begin{align*} (2-\lambda)(4-\lambda)-(-1) &= 0,\\ \lambda^2 -6 \lambda + 9 &= 0, \\ \end{align*} $$ and so $\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = 3$ is a repeated eigenvalue. We have $$\mathbf{M}_1 = \mathbf{A}-\lambda_1 \mathbf{I} = \mathbf{A}-3\mathbf{I} = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} -1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{array} \right].$$ We have $$ p_1' = 3p_1, \qquad p_1(0) = 1$$ and so $$p_1(t) = e^{3t}.$$ The ODE for $p_2$ is $$p_2' - 3p_2 = e^{3t}, \qquad p_2(0) = 0.$$ The complementary solution is $p_{2,c} = c_1 e^{3t}$, and our undetermined coefficient guess for a particular solution is then $p_{2,p} = ate^{3t}$ due to matchup with the complementary solution. Then, $$ (ae^{3t} + 3ate^{3t}) - 3ate^{3t} = e^{3t},$$ and so our undetermined coefficient is $a=1$. Therefore, $$p_2 = p_{2,c} + p_{2,p} = c_1 e^{3t} + te^{3t},$$ and so $p_2(0) = 0$ means $c_1 = 0$ and therefore $$p_2(t) = te^{3t}.$$ Putting it all together, $$ \begin{align*} e^{\mathbf{A}t} &= p_1(t) \mathbf{M}_0 + p_2(t) \mathbf{M}_1 \\ \\ &= e^{3t} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] + te^{3t} \left[ \begin{array}{cc} -1 & 1 \\ -1 & 1 \end{array} \right] \\ \\ &= \left[ \begin{array}{cc} e^{3t} - te^{3t} & te^{3t} \\ -te^{3t} & e^{3t} + te^{3t} \end{array} \right] \end{align*} $$ and the general solution to $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{A} \mathbf{x}$ is $$ \mathbf{x}(t) =e^{\mathbf{A}t}\mathbf{c} = \left[ \begin{array}{cc} e^{3t} - te^{3t} & te^{3t} \\ -te^{3t} & e^{3t} + te^{3t} \end{array} \right]\left[ \begin{array}{c} c_1 \\ c_2 \end{array} \right] = c_1 \left[ \begin{array}{c} e^{3t} - te^{3t} \\ -te^{3t} \end{array} \right] + c_2 \left[ \begin{array}{c} te^{3t} \\ e^{3t} + te^{3t} \end{array} \right].$$

For $n=3$, there is one more step to the algorithm. We have $$e^{\mathbf{A}t} = p_1(t)\mathbf{M}_0 + p_2(t) \mathbf{M}_1 + p_3(t) \mathbf{M}_2$$ where $$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{M}_0 &= \mathbf{I}, \\ \\ \mathbf{M}_1 &= \prod_{k=1}^1 (\mathbf{A}-\lambda_k \mathbf{I}) = (\mathbf{A}-\lambda_1\mathbf{I}),\\ \\ \mathbf{M}_2 &= \prod_{k=1}^2 (\mathbf{A}-\lambda_k \mathbf{I}) = (\mathbf{A}-\lambda_1\mathbf{I})(\mathbf{A}-\lambda_2\mathbf{I}),\\ \\ p_1' &= \lambda_1 p_1, \quad \quad \quad p_1(0)=1, \\ p_2' &= p_1 + \lambda_2 p_2, \quad p_2(0) = 0,\\ p_3' &= p_2 + \lambda_3 p_3, \quad p_3(0) = 0. \end{align*} $$ First we find and label the eigenvalues, the roots to $\det(\mathbf{A}-\lambda \mathbf{I} ) = 0$: $$ \begin{align*} \det \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 - \lambda & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1-\lambda & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 -\lambda \end{array}\right] &= 0, \\ \\ (1-\lambda) \det \left[\begin{array}{cc} 1-\lambda & 0 \\ 1 & 1-\lambda \end{array} \right] - (1) \det\left[\begin{array}{cc} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1-\lambda \end{array} \right] + 0 \det \left[\begin{array}{cc} 0 & 1-\lambda \\ 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] &= 0, \\ \\ (1-\lambda)^3 &= 0,\\ \end{align*} $$ and so $\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = \lambda_3 = 1$ is a triple eigenvalue. We have $$ \begin{align*} \mathbf{M}_1 &= \mathbf{A} - \lambda_1 \mathbf{I} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right],\\ \\ \mathbf{M}_2 &= (\mathbf{A}-\lambda_1 \mathbf{I})(\mathbf{A}-\lambda_2\mathbf{I}) = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right]\left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right] = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right], \end{align*} $$ and so we won't need to calculate $p_3(t)$. Since $$p_1' = p_1, \qquad p_1(0) = 1$$ we have $$p_1(t) = e^t.$$ Then, we have $$p_2' - p_2 = e^t, \qquad p_2(0) = 0$$ and so the complementary solution is $p_{2,c} = c_1e^t$. The particular solution is of the form $p_{2,p} = ate^t$ (due to overlap with the complementary solution), and substituting in yields $$(ae^t + ate^t) - ate^t = e^t,$$ and so $a=1$ and $$p_2 = p_{2_c} + p_{2,p} = c_1e^t + te^t,$$ and $p_2(0) = 0$ means $c_1 = 0$ and therefore $$p_2(t) = te^t.$$ Putting it together, $$ \begin{align*} e^{\mathbf{A}t} &= p_1(t) \mathbf{M}_0 + p_2(t) \mathbf{M}_1 + p_3(t) \mathbf{M}_2 \\ \\ &= e^t \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] + te^t \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{array} \right]+ p_3(t) \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array} \right] \\ \\ &= \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} e^t & te^t & 0 \\ 0 & e^t & 0 \\ 0 & te^t & e^t \end{array} \right]. \end{align*} $$ The general solution of $\mathbf{x}' = \mathbf{A}\mathbf{x}$ is then $$ \mathbf{x}(t) =e^{\mathbf{A}t}\mathbf{c} = c_1 \left[ \begin{array}{c} e^t \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{array} \right] + c_2 \left[ \begin{array}{c} te^t \\ e^t \\ te^t \end{array} \right] + c_3 \left[ \begin{array}{c} 0 \\ 0 \\ e^t \end{array} \right].$$

(ii)¶

We have the bounds $|\cos(t)| \leq 1, |\sin(t)| \leq 1, |\cos(\sqrt{3}t)| \leq 1, |\sin(\sqrt{3}t)| \leq 1$ for all $t$.

Also, $\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{6} \leq \frac 1 2$ and $\frac 1 2 + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \leq \frac 3 2$.

Along with the triangle inequality $|a+b| \leq |a| + |b|$, we can see

$$ \begin{align*} |u| &= |x_1| \leq |c_1| + |c_2| + |c_3| + |c_4|,\\ \\ |u'| &= |x_2| \leq \frac 3 2 |c_1| + |c_2| + \frac 3 2 |c_3| + |c_4|, \\ \\ |v| &= |x_3| \leq |c_1| + |c_2| + |c_3| + |c_4|,\\ \\ |v'| &= |x_4| \leq \frac 3 2 |c_1| + |c_2| + \frac 3 2 |c_3| + |c_4|. \end{align*} $$

Therefore, for any $c_1, c_2, c_3, c_4$ we have that each component of $\mathbf{x}(t)$ is bounded for all $t$; and so the trivial solution $\mathbf{x}(t) \equiv \mathbf{0}$ is stable.

The precise definition of an equilibrium solution $\mathbf{x}^\ast$ being stable is that for any $\epsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ so that $$ \| \mathbf{x}(0) - \mathbf{x}^\ast \| < \delta \implies \| \mathbf{x}(t) - \mathbf{x}^\ast \| < \epsilon \text{ for all } t > 0.$$

That is, for any distance from the equilibrium $\mathbf{x}^\ast$ we wish to stay within, we can always find a "closeness threshold" $\delta$ so that all initial conditions within $\delta$ of $\mathbf{x}^\ast$ will yield solutions that stay within $\epsilon$ of $\mathbf{x}^\ast$ for all $t > 0$.

It turns out the eigenvalues of this matrix are $\pm i, \pm \sqrt{3} i$, which are purely imaginary and distinct - meaning there is no exponential growth (would come from positive real part eigenvalues), or power growth (if $\pm i$ were repeated for example, we would have terms $t \cos t$ and $t \sin t$ which would grow as $t \to + \infty$.)

This means that the motion of the system "stays under control" --- any solutions will have the displacements and velocities bounded in terms of the initial displacements and velocities. There is no exponential growth (which would require "negative, nonphysical" friction strength), or any resonance/self-reinforcement between the masses in for these parameters.

(iii)¶

In problem 2.35, you might have noticed that the matrix exponential solution looks a little more complicated than the solutions that the eigenvector method would have produced.

However, one advantage of the matrix exponential approach is that when writing the general solution as $$ \mathbf{x}(t) = e^{\mathbf{A}t} \mathbf{c},$$ we have that the constants are just the initial values, since $e^{\mathbf{0}} = \mathbf{I}$. Therefore, we have $$ \mathbf{x}(t) = e^{\mathbf{A}t} \mathbf{x}(0).$$

For $\mathbf{x}(0) = [1, 0, 0, 0]^T$, we have

$$\mathbf{x}(t) = \left[ \begin{matrix} \frac 1 2 (\cos t + \cos \sqrt{3} t) \\ - \frac 1 2 (\sin t + \sqrt{3} \sin \sqrt{3} t) \\ \frac 1 2 (\cos t - \cos \sqrt{3} t) \\ - \frac 1 2 (\sin t - \sqrt{3} \sin \sqrt{3} t) \end{matrix} \right].$$

Since the frequencies $\sqrt{3}$ and $1$ have an irrational ratio, these functions are not periodic!

However, our analysis in (ii) shows they are all bounded.

Chaos is characterized by solutions behaving uncontrollably (not repeating exactly) in a bounded part of phase space. However, this motion is not chaotic --- nearby initial conditions do not exponentially diverge from one another, as would be the case for the chaotic nonlinear double pendulum equations. Math 417 discusses the qualitiative theory of nonlinear ODE systems, and it turns out chaos can only happen in nonlinear ODE systems with at least 3 dependent variables. (The Poincare-Bendixson theorm asserts that "there isn't 'enough room' in a phase plane for solutions to behave chaotically" --- a minimum of 3 dimensions in the phase space is needed. However, chaos is possible in 1-dimensional discrete nonlinear dynamical systems though --- see the classic example of the logistic map.)

(ii)¶

As can be seen above, each component of $\mathbf{x}(t)$ is a linear combination of $e^{-t}, te^{-t}, e^{-t}\sin(\sqrt{2} t),$ and $e^{-t} \cos(\sqrt{2} t).$

Clearly,

$$\lim_{t \to \infty} e^{-t} = 0,$$

and

$$\lim_{t \to \infty} te^{-t} = \lim_{t \to \infty} \frac{t}{e^t} = 0$$

by L'Hospital's rule. We also have $\displaystyle \lim_{t \to \infty} e^{-t} \cos(\sqrt{2} t) = 0$ by the Squeeze Thoerem, since

$$ - e^{-t} \leq e^{-t} \cos(\sqrt{2} t) \leq e^{-t} \quad \text{ for all } t,$$

and similarly $\displaystyle \lim_{t \to \infty} e^{-t} \sin(\sqrt{2}t) = 0.$

It follows that for any $c_1, c_2, c_3, c_4$ that

$$\lim_{t \to \infty} \mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{0},$$

and so the origin $\mathbf{x}^\ast = \mathbf{0}$ is a globally asymptotically stable equilibrium solution.

This means that the friction in the system causes both mass positions to approach their equilibrium positions, with velocities also tending to zero.

(It turns out the eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = -1, \lambda_{3,4} = -1 \pm \sqrt{2} i.$ All the real parts are strictly negative, which guarantees global asymptotic stability for a linear system.)
