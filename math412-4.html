1. Maximum Principle

Slide 2

Suppose \(u\) solves Laplace's equation on the interior of some closed, bounded region \(R\).

The extreme value theorem states that continuous functions on closed, bounded (compact) domains must attain absolute maximum values somewhere in the domain.

M230 approach to finding absolute max/min of \(f(x,y)\) on closed, bounded region \(R\):
1) Look at critical points inside \(R\) where \(\nabla f = 0\).
2) Compare with max/min on the boundary.

Since solutions to Laplace's equation cannot have strict local maxima or minima in the interior (as shown in the previous lecture), the absolute maximum must occur on the boundary:

Maximum Principle. \[ \max_{(x,y)\in R} u(x,y) = \max_{(x,y)\text{ on boundary}} u(x,y). \] We can similarly establish a minimum principle.

2. BVP with Prescribed Value Is Well-Posed for Laplace's Equation

Slides 3–4

We consider the Dirichlet BVP:

\[ \begin{cases} \nabla^2 u = 0 & (x,y) \in R \\[6pt] u(x,y) = f(x,y) & (x,y) \text{ on boundary} \end{cases} \]

Uniqueness

Suppose \(u_1 \not\equiv u_2\) are two solutions. Then \(w := u_1 - u_2\) is a nontrivial solution with homogeneous boundary data.

\(\nabla^2 w = 0\) and \(w = f - f = 0\) on the boundary.

However, the maximum and minimum principles guarantee that for any \((x,y) \in R\):

\[ 0 = \min_{(x,y)\text{ on bdry}}\bigl(u_1(x,y) - u_2(x,y)\bigr) \;\le\; \bigl(u_1(x,y) - u_2(x,y)\bigr) \;\le\; \max_{(x,y)\text{ on bdry}}\bigl(u_1(x,y) - u_2(x,y)\bigr) = 0, \]
The left inequality uses the minimum principle; the right uses the maximum principle. Since the boundary data is zero, both the max and min on the boundary are zero.

so \(u_1 - u_2 \equiv 0\), i.e., \(u_1 \equiv u_2\). Contradiction — hence the solution is unique.

Continuous dependence

Let \(u_1\) solve the problem with data \(f_1\), and \(u_2\) solve with data \(f_2\). The maximum principle applied to \((u_1 - u_2)\) with data \((f_1 - f_2)\) asserts:

\[ \max_{(x,y)\in R}\bigl|u_1(x,y) - u_2(x,y)\bigr| \;\le\; \max_{(x,y)\text{ on boundary}}\bigl|f_1(x,y) - f_2(x,y)\bigr|, \]
This uses both the max principle and the min principle applied to \(|u_1 - u_2|\). The max of \(|u_1-u_2|\) over the whole region is bounded by the max of \(|u_1-u_2|\) on the boundary.

so small changes in data cause small changes in solutions!

3. Existence

Slide 5

We've shown how to construct solutions for rectangles and disks using separation of variables.

A similar argument to the disk can be done for the unbounded region outside the disk: choose \(r^{-n}\) solutions to enforce boundedness as \(r \to \infty\). This has interesting applications to irrotational incompressible fluid flow.

For the annulus (homework), use both \(r^{+n}\) and \(r^{-n}\) — you have "twice as many" coefficients to solve for, but there will be boundary data on two circles.

Existence for more complicated domains can be addressed by a variety of constructive and nonconstructive methods: conformal maps, method of images, Green's functions, etc. — essentially anything used to construct gravitational/electrical potentials and potential flows.

See Ch. 9 for Green's functions and related methods.

4. Other Kinds of Data: Solvability Conditions & Nonuniqueness

Slide 6

For the heat equation, we've discussed three types of boundary conditions: prescribed temperature, prescribed heat flux, and Newton's law of cooling (a linear combination of the first two).

Recall: heat flux \(\vec{\phi} = -K_0 \nabla u\), so prescribing flux means prescribing \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\) at the boundary. Newton's law of cooling is a "convective" BC (constant temperature environment).
Easy 1D Heat Equation Fact. The steady-state problem \[ \pdd{u}{x} = 0, \qquad \pd{u}{x}(0) = a, \qquad \pd{u}{x}(L) = b \] has steady-state solutions if and only if \(a = b\). In that case, they are of the form \(u(x) = ax + c\), where \(c\) is any constant.
At steady state, \(u(x,t) = u(x)\) (no time dependence), so \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = 0\) and the PDE becomes \(u'' = 0\).
Physical reasoning: define the total heat energy \(E(t) = \int_0^L u(x,t)\,dx\). Then \(E'(t) = \int_0^L \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}\,dx = \int_0^L k\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\,dx = k\bigl[\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\bigr]_0^L = k(b - a)\). So \(E'(t) \equiv 0 \iff a = b\). This is the "solvability condition" for the 1D prescribed-derivative Laplace equation.

5. Laplace BVP with Prescribed Normal Derivative

Slide 7

The analog of "prescribed heat flux" for multiple space dimensions is the Neumann problem:

\[ \begin{cases} \nabla^2 u = 0 & (x,y) \in R \\[6pt] \nabla u \cdot \hat{n}\,\Big|_{(x,y)} = f(x,y) & (x,y) \text{ on boundary} \end{cases} \]
\(D_{\hat{n}} u = \nabla u \cdot \hat{n}\) is the rate of heat leaving the region at each boundary point. Here \(\hat{n}\) is the outward unit normal vector of the boundary.
Steady-state solution ⇒ total heat energy in \(R\) remains constant. So net heat flux across boundary must be zero.

Since the 2D divergence theorem ("Green's theorem for flux line integrals") states:

\[ 0 = \iint_R \nabla^2 u\,dA = \iint_R \nabla\cdot(\nabla u)\,dA = \oint_{\text{boundary}} \nabla u \cdot \hat{n}\,ds = \oint_{\text{boundary}} f\,ds, \]
Chain of equalities: (1) \(\nabla^2 u = 0\) by the PDE; (2) \(\nabla^2 u = \nabla \cdot (\nabla u)\) — definition of Laplacian; (3) 2D divergence theorem; (4) boundary condition substitution.

we only have steady-state solutions if the "net prescribed flux" is zero.

On a rectangle, the outward normal on the boundary sides gives: \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\) on top/bottom, \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\) on left/right (with appropriate signs). The divergence theorem relates the "work" line integral \(\oint \vec{F}\cdot d\vec{r}\) (tangent direction along \(C\)) to the flux integral.

6. Boundary Data Types

Slide 8
Boundary condition terminology (for heat/Laplace equations):

Type 1 — Dirichlet data: Prescribed value. ("prescribed temperature")

Type 2 — Neumann data: Prescribed normal derivative. ("prescribed flux")

Type 3 — Robin data: A linear combination of both. (Newton's law of cooling)

Cauchy data: Initial conditions.

Fact (Neumann BVP). Provided \[ \oint_{\text{boundary}} f\,ds = 0 \qquad\textbf{(compatibility/solvability condition)}, \] the Neumann problem \[ \begin{cases} \nabla^2 u = 0 & (x,y) \in R \\ \nabla u \cdot \hat{n}\big|_{(x,y)} = f(x,y) & (x,y) \text{ on boundary} \end{cases} \] has a one-parameter family of solutions indexed by an additive constant.
Net heat energy entering/leaving \(R\) must be zero — that's the solvability condition. If \(u(x,y)\) solves the Neumann BVP, so does \(u + C\) for any constant \(C\)! (Since \(\nabla(u+C) = \nabla u\) and \(\nabla^2(u+C) = \nabla^2 u = 0\).)

7. Fredholm Theory Connection

Slide 9

There is a deep connection between having a solvability condition and nonuniqueness of the homogeneous problem. This is analogous to the concepts of free variables/ranges and kernels from Math 220 (linear algebra).

In this Laplace BVP context, the infinite-dimensional linear operator is Fredholm (infinite-dimensional linear algebra / functional analysis) — which means it behaves similarly to finite-dimensional linear operators (Math 220, 441, 436). See Section 9.4 for more.

8. Energy Arguments for Heat Equation Well-Posedness

Slides 10–11

There are mean value and maximum principles for the heat equation that can also establish uniqueness and well-posedness, but they are more difficult to establish than the analogs for Laplace's equation.

Instead we use a (mathematical) energy method. Often these "mathematical energies" coincide with physical energies, but they are a widely applicable technique in PDE.

Setting: \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\), \(t > 0\), \(0 < x < L\), with homogeneous boundary conditions (any column on the reference sheet).

The energy functional

Assume \(u(x,t)\) solves the heat equation with homogeneous boundary conditions (zero temperature ends, insulated ends, or periodic). Define:

\[ E(t) := \frac{1}{2}\int_0^L u(x,t)^2\,dx \;\ge\; 0, \qquad\text{with } E = 0 \iff u(\cdot,t) \equiv 0. \]
\(E(t)\) is proportional to the square of the \(L^2\) norm of \(u(x,t)\). It is "positive definite" (zero only when \(u \equiv 0\)) — this is the "coercive" requirement of norms.

Computing \(E'(t)\)

\[ \begin{aligned} E'(t) &= \int_0^L u(x,t)\,\pd{u}{t}(x,t)\,dx \\[8pt] &\stackrel{\text{PDE}}{=} k\int_0^L u(x,t)\,\pdd{u}{x}(x,t)\,dx \\[8pt] &\stackrel{\text{int. by parts}}{=} k\,u(x,t)\,\pd{u}{x}(x,t)\bigg|_0^L - k\int_0^L \left(\pd{u}{x}\right)^2 dx \\[8pt] &\le 0. \end{aligned} \]
Integration by parts: \(\mathcal{U} = u\), \(d\mathcal{V} = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}\,dx\), so \(d\mathcal{U} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\,dx\) and \(\mathcal{V} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\).
The boundary term \(k\,u\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\big|_0^L = 0\) for all three types of homogeneous BCs:
• Zero temperature: \(u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0\).
• Insulated: \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(0,t) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(L,t) = 0\).
• Periodic: \(u(0,t) = u(L,t)\) and \(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(0,t) = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(L,t)\), so boundary terms cancel.

The remaining integral \(-k\int_0^L (\frac{\partial u}{\partial x})^2\,dx \le 0\) since the integrand is non-negative and \(k > 0\).

Therefore:

\[ \boxed{\;E(t) \le E(0).\;} \]

The energy is non-increasing in time.

9. Uniqueness & Continuous Dependence for Heat Equation IBVP

Slides 12–14

Consider the IBVP with inhomogeneous (prescribed temperature) boundary conditions:

\[ \begin{cases} \pd{u}{t} = k\,\pdd{u}{x}, & t > 0,\; 0 < x < L \\[6pt] u(0,t) = g(t), & t > 0 \\[4pt] u(L,t) = h(t), & t > 0 \\[4pt] u(x,0) = f(x), & 0 < x < L \end{cases} \]
Prescribed temperature BCs (Dirichlet type, possibly time-dependent).

This problem has uniqueness — at most one solution exists.

Proof of uniqueness

Let \(u_1 \not\equiv u_2\) be two solutions, and define \(w := u_1 - u_2\). Then \(w\) solves the heat equation (the PDE is linear homogeneous) with homogeneous boundary AND initial conditions:

\(w\) satisfies: \(\frac{\partial w}{\partial t} = k\frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x^2}\), with \(w(0,t) = w(L,t) = 0\) and \(w(x,0) = 0\).

Apply the energy estimate:

\[ 0 \;\le\; E(t) \;\le\; E(0) = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^L w(x,0)^2\,dx = 0, \]
Positive definiteness: \(E(t) \ge 0\).
Energy decay: \(E(t) \le E(0)\).
Combined: \(E(t) = 0\) for all \(t\), so \(w(\cdot,t) \equiv 0\) for all \(t\), hence \(w \equiv 0\), i.e., \(u_1 \equiv u_2\). Contradiction!

Continuous dependence on initial data

Let \(u_1\) solve with IC \(f_1\), and \(u_2\) solve with IC \(f_2\) (same BCs). Then \(E(t) \le E(0)\) applied to \(w = u_1 - u_2\) gives:

\[ \int_0^L \bigl(u_1(x,t) - u_2(x,t)\bigr)^2\,dx \;\le\; \int_0^L \bigl(f_1(x) - f_2(x)\bigr)^2\,dx. \]

For initial conditions "close with respect to the \(L^2\) distance between functions," the solutions for all \(t > 0\) are also close with respect to the same notion of distance.

Comparison of distance notions. For Laplace's equation, the continuous dependence estimate was: \[ \max_{(x,y)\in R}\bigl|u_1 - u_2\bigr| \;\le\; \max_{(x,y)\text{ on bdry}}\bigl|f_1 - f_2\bigr|, \] which is the "uniform" or \(L^\infty\) notion of distance. For the heat equation, the energy method gives the analogous statement with respect to the \(L^2\) distance.

10. Power of Qualitative Methods

Slide 15

Often energy methods involve equations/inequalities between \(E'(t)\) and \(E(t)\), which can be used to argue uniqueness and continuous dependence. In addition, a priori estimates/energy estimates for constructed approximate solutions can often be used to argue existence.

Key insight: Notice how powerful these qualitative methods (mean value property, maximum principle, energy methods) are for establishing powerful conclusions without working with solution formulas!

Since solution formulas are really only readily available for constant-coefficient linear problems, these qualitative techniques are even more important for variable-coefficient and nonlinear PDE.
