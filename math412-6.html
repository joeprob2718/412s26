Deriving the 1D Wave Equation for a Vertically Vibrating String

We consider a horizontally tightly stretched string with known mass density (per length).

"Tightly stretched" means the ends are tied down in some way (to be made more precise later) so that there is a horizontal equilibrium configuration around which we will measure perturbations.

Let $\alpha$ be the point on the string located at $x = \alpha$ when in equilibrium, so that this point on the string is located at $$\bigl(x(\alpha, t),\; y(\alpha, t)\bigr)$$ at time $t$.

At equilibrium, the piece labeled $\alpha$ is located at $(\alpha, 0)$.

2/20

We have $$\langle v(\alpha,t),\, u(\alpha,t)\rangle = \langle x(\alpha,t) - \alpha,\; y(\alpha,t) - 0 \rangle$$ is the displacement vector from equilibrium.

We assume the slope of the string is small, so that the horizontal displacement $v$ can be neglected and the motion is entirely vertical, thus $x = \alpha$, and we can track the string entirely by

$$y = y(\alpha, t) = y(x, t) = \boxed{u(x,t)}.$$

For the 1D wave equation we'll derive from force balance, the unknown function is $$u(x, t)$$ which is the vertical displacement (from equilibrium) of the point on the string located at horizontal position $x$ at time $t$.

3/20

Slope, Tension, and Force Balance

Let $$\left.\frac{dy}{dx}\right|_{(x,t)} = \left.\tan\theta\right|_{(x,t)} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(x,t) \quad \text{(dimensionless)}$$ be the slope of the string (so $\theta$ is the angle from horizontal).

$\theta(x,t)$: elevation angle of string at position $x$, time $t$.

We assume the string is perfectly flexible so that the only force the string exerts on itself is in the tangential direction with magnitude $$T(x,t) > 0 \;\text{N}$$ where $T$ is the tension.

$T(x,t)$ is the length of the tension force vector (along the string).
Vertical projection of tension force at right end of a small piece; picture case with $\theta > 0$ on this piece.

4/20

We consider the force balance equation (Newton's second law) for a small segment of string:

$$m\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{F}.$$
$\rho(x)$: linear density $\left(\frac{\text{kg}}{\text{m}}\right)$ along string.

The horizontal component can be neglected (small slope assumption) and the vertical component is

$$\underbrace{\rho(x)\,\Delta x}_{\text{mass of small piece}} \;\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = \underbrace{T(x+\Delta x,t)\sin\theta(x+\Delta x,t)}_{\uparrow} - \underbrace{T(x,t)\sin\theta(x,t)}_{\downarrow} + \rho(x)\Delta x\, Q(x,t),$$
Left side: mass × vertical acceleration. E.g. gravity: $Q(x,t) = -9.8\;\frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}^2}$.

where $Q(x,t)$ is the (vertical) body force (gravitational, electromagnetic, …) per unit mass. Divide by $\Delta x$ and take the limit to $0$ to obtain

$$\rho(x)\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\bigl(T(x,t)\sin\theta(x,t)\bigr) + \rho(x)\,Q(x,t).$$
Note: $\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \tan\theta \neq \sin\theta$ in general.

5/20

Perfect Elastic Assumption + Small Slope

Think of a rubber band!

Real strings are nearly perfectly elastic, meaning $T(x,t)$ depends only on the local stretching of the string. However, this "local stretching" satisfies

$$\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2} - 1 \le \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2$$
This measures the stretching. The length of a small piece is $\sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2} = \Delta x\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\right)^2}$, versus the equilibrium length $\Delta x$.

using the Mean Value Theorem, and this is second order in our "small slope".

More precisely, for $x \ge 0$, $$\sqrt{1+x} - 1 = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{1+z}}(x - 0) \le \frac{1}{2}x$$ for some $0 < z < x$.

6/20

Therefore, our "small slope" assumption — $\dfrac{dy}{dx} \approx 0$, ignoring anything second order or higher in $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ — leads to assuming the tension is constant:

$$T(x,t) \equiv T_0.$$

This small slope assumption also allows us to approximate

$$\sin\theta(x,t) \approx \tan\theta(x,t) = \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial x},$$

since assuming $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \tan\theta$ is small is equivalent to assuming $\theta$ is small since $\tan\theta$ and $\theta$ agree to first order near $\theta = 0$, and then $\sin\theta \approx \tan\theta$ for $\theta \approx 0$.

Taylor expansions: $\sin\theta = \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{6} + \cdots$, $\tan\theta = \theta - \frac{\theta^3}{3} + \cdots$. These agree to first order.
For non-small angles: $\sin\theta(x,t) = \cos\theta \cdot \tan\theta$, so $T(x,t) = T\!\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right)$ or $T\!\left(\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right)^2} - 1\right)$.

7/20

The 1D Wave Equation

Therefore, substituting $T(x,t) \equiv T_0$ and $\sin\theta \approx \frac{\partial u}{\partial x}$:

$$\rho(x)\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(T(x,t)\sin\theta(x,t)\right) + \rho(x)\,Q(x,t)$$

leads to

$$\rho(x)\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = T_0 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} + \rho(x)\,Q(x,t).$$
The body force $Q$ is crossed out: often negligible relative to the tensile force.

For $Q(x,t) = -g$ (or other similar scenarios), we often have this force is negligible relative to the tensile force, and so we have

$$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = \frac{T_0}{\rho(x)}\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}.$$

In the case where $\rho(x) = \rho_0$ is constant, we have the 1D wave equation:

$$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$$
$\dfrac{T_0}{\rho_0} =: c^2$, where $c^2$ has units of speed squared. It represents the traveling wave speed for the wave equation.

8/20

Boundary Conditions

Like guitar or piano strings!

Like our heat equation / Laplace (steady heat) equation problems, we need boundary conditions.

For a string occupying $0 \le x \le L$, at either end (i.e., at $x = 0$ or $x = L$) we can have:

  • Prescribed displacement: $u(\cdot, t) = f(t)$ or fixed at zero displacement: $u(\cdot, t) = 0$.
  • Free end: attached to frictionless vertically moving track: $$T_0\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(\cdot, t) = 0 \quad \text{(or prescribed angle at end)}$$
  • Attached to mass-spring system with variable support: $$m\frac{d^2}{dt^2}u(\cdot, t) = -k\bigl(u(\cdot, t) - y_s(t) - l\bigr) + T_0\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}(\cdot, t)$$
The bottom of the spring support $y_s(t)$ is known.

9/20

Vibrating String with Fixed Ends

We consider

$$\begin{cases} \dfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2\dfrac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} & 0 < x < L,\; t > 0 \\[8pt] u(0, t) = 0 & t > 0 \\[4pt] u(L, t) = 0 & t > 0 \\[4pt] u(x, 0) = f(x) & 0 \le x \le L \\[4pt] \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x, 0) = g(x) & 0 \le x \le L \end{cases}$$
$u(x,0) = f(x)$: initial shape of string.
$\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x,0) = g(x)$: initial vertical velocity along string.

Second-order ODEs require initial value and initial derivative; for this second-order time PDE (like all Newton's law situations, ODE or PDE) we need to know the initial position and velocity of each segment of string to have any hope of predicting the future.

Separation of Variables

As usual, the product solution ansatz is

$$u_p(x,t) = \phi(x)\,h(t)$$
Substituting: $\phi\, h'' = c^2 \phi''\, h$. Divide by $\phi\, h\, c^2$.

leading to

$$\frac{1}{c^2}\frac{h''(t)}{h(t)} = \frac{\phi''(x)}{\phi(x)} =: -\lambda.$$

The separated ODE system is then

$$\begin{cases} \phi''(x) = -\lambda\,\phi(x), & \phi(0) = \phi(L) = 0 \\[6pt] h''(t) = -\lambda\, c^2\, h(t) \end{cases}$$
This is the same $\phi$ BVP we've seen all semester! (Laplace's equation eigenvalue problem: $\phi'' = -\lambda\phi$.)
$\phi$: $\cos/\sin$ when $\lambda > 0$. $h$: $\cosh/\sinh$ when $\lambda < 0$ — but $h'' = -\lambda c^2 h$ with $\lambda > 0$ gives $\cos/\sin$ for $h$ too.

The only nontrivial solutions are

$$\phi(x) = B\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \quad \text{corresponding to } \lambda = \left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^2 > 0.$$

11/20

$h'' + \left(\frac{n\pi c}{L}\right)^2 h = 0$

Since the structure of the wave equation means that $h$ "solves the exact same sort of ODE" (with separation constant dilated by $c^2 > 0$) we have

$$h(t) = c_1\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi c\, t}{L}\right) + c_2\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi c\, t}{L}\right)$$

and superimposed solutions

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(A_n \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\cos\frac{n\pi c\, t}{L} + B_n \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\sin\frac{n\pi c\, t}{L}\right).$$
Inhomogeneous conditions: $u(x,0) = f(x)$ and $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}(x,0) = g(x)$.
At $t = 0$: the $\cos$ terms become $1$ and the $\sin$ terms become $0$, giving $u(x,0) = \sum A_n \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L} = f(x)$.
Computing $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t}$: the $\cos\frac{n\pi ct}{L}$ differentiates to $-\frac{n\pi c}{L}\sin\frac{n\pi ct}{L}$ (which vanishes at $t=0$), and $\sin\frac{n\pi ct}{L}$ differentiates to $\frac{n\pi c}{L}\cos\frac{n\pi ct}{L}$ (which is $\frac{n\pi c}{L}$ at $t=0$).

12/20

Initial conditions require

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} A_n \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}$$ $$g(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\underbrace{B_n \frac{n\pi c}{L}}\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}.$$

Usual orthogonality relations lead to

$$A_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\,dx$$ $$B_n = \frac{2}{n\pi c}\int_0^L g(x)\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\,dx.$$
Use the orthogonality: $\frac{2}{L}\int_0^L g(x)\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\,dx = B_n\frac{n\pi c}{L}$, so solve for $B_n$.
$\omega$ is angular frequency (rad/sec). One product solution: $X\cos(\omega t) + Y\sin(\omega t) = R\cos(\omega t - \Theta)$, where $(X,Y) \to (R,\Theta)$ in polar coordinates.

13/20

Normal Modes & Stringed Instrument Interpretation

Vertical displacement is a linear combination of simple product solutions, or normal modes of vibration/oscillation:

$$\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\left(A_n\cos\frac{n\pi c\,t}{L} + B_n\sin\frac{n\pi c\,t}{L}\right) = R_n\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi c\,t}{L} - \Theta_n\right).$$
$R_n$: amplitude. $\Theta_n$: phase delay angle.

Here we combined the time cosine–sine sum (with same frequency) into a single phase-shifted cosine with amplitude $R_n = \sqrt{A_n^2 + B_n^2}$ and "phase delay" $\Theta_n$ which satisfies $\tan\Theta_n = \dfrac{B_n}{A_n}$.

The time-frequency (in Hz if $t$ has units seconds) of oscillations is

$$f = \frac{\left(\frac{n\pi c}{L}\right)}{2\pi} = \frac{nc}{2L} = \frac{n\sqrt{T_0}}{2L\sqrt{\rho_0}}.$$
$2\pi$ radians = 1 cycle. Hz = "cycles/sec" = $\frac{1}{\text{sec}}$.

$n = 1$ is the first harmonic or fundamental, and the other natural frequencies are integer multiples of it.

14/20

Instruments are tuned by adjusting the tension $T_0$; guitars, violins, etc. are played by adjusting $L$, and they need a "fresh setup" if you change string gauge (adjust $\rho_0$ — so to get the same tuning you'd need to change $T_0$).

The timbre of an instrument is determined by the typical amplitudes $\{R_n\} = \{R_1, R_2, R_3, \ldots\}$ of the modes produced by typical initial/forcing conditions.

Standing wave = spatial profile × oscillation: $f(x,t) = F(x)\,G(t)$.

15/20

Standing Waves

If we think of each normal mode as

$$\underbrace{R_n\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi c\,t}{L} - \Theta_n\right)}_{\text{time-dependent amplitude}} \cdot \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L},$$

we have a simple oscillation in $x$ whose amplitude varies periodically in time — which we call a standing wave.

All modes are $0$ at $x = 0, L$;

  • The $n = 2$ (second harmonic, an octave up) mode has a node at $x = \dfrac{L}{2}$.
  • The $n = 3$ (third harmonic, a perfect fifth!) mode has nodes at $x = \dfrac{L}{3},\;\dfrac{2L}{3}$; …

Traveling Waves

A function $u(x,t)$ is a traveling wave with velocity $+c$ (so moving right) if it is of the form

$$u(x,t) := R(x - ct).$$
$c > 0$: wave moves to the right.

The functions $u(\cdot, t)$ are all horizontal shifts of $u(\cdot, 0)$; it describes time-dependence of "keeping the same exact shape, sliding with constant velocity $+c$".

$u(x,0) = R(x)$ at $t=0$; $u(x,1) = R(x-c)$ shifted right by $c$; $u(x,2) = R(x-2c)$ shifted right by $2c$.

17/20

Additionally, using the product-to-sum identity we have

$$R_n\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L}\cos\!\left(\frac{n\pi c\,t}{L} - \Theta_n\right) = \frac{R_n}{2}\left[\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi}{L}(x - ct) + \Theta_n\right) + \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi}{L}(x + ct) - \Theta_n\right)\right]$$ $$=: R(x - ct) + S(x + ct),$$
$R(x-ct)$: right-moving wave. $S(x+ct)$: left-moving wave.

so each term (and therefore the full solution) is a linear combination of just two traveling waves — one with velocity $+c$ and one with velocity $-c$.

Each normal mode (different time frequency, each term) decomposes into traveling waves. BUT: $\sum(\text{normal modes}) \neq \text{standing wave}$ in general. However, $\sum(\text{normal modes}) = \sum R_n(x-ct) + \sum S_n(x+ct)$.

18/20

We would call $c$ the "speed of vibration propagation (sound?) of the medium" (so the string), which is not the familiar sound speed $c \approx 343$ m/s for air.

$c = \sqrt{\dfrac{T_0}{\rho_0}}$ has units m/s.

The 2D wave equation governs drums, membranes, etc. (would set up force due to surface tension in this case).

The 3D wave equation with $c \approx 343$ m/s arises from linearizing the compressible Euler (no friction) fluid equations and governs the behavior of small pressure variations in the air — so it's the law of the land for all the "sound" we hear!

In addition, Maxwell's equations are a system of 6 PDE in $(x, y, z, t)$ that govern the three magnetic and electric field components in electromagnetism. The wave equation can be discovered in these more complicated equations, and in that case $c \approx 3 \times 10^8$ m/s is the speed of light (which is defined in terms of the "free space physical parameters" $\mu_0, \epsilon_0$ in Maxwell's equations).
