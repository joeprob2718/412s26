Quick DE & ODE Review
Differential equation: An equation involving an unknown function and its derivative(s).

For ordinary differential equations (ODEs), the unknown dependent variable is a function of a single independent variable. Most often we think of this as time \(t\), so the ODE describes a dynamical system.

Examples from Math 250/251

Exponential growth / decay
\[ x' = ax \implies x(t) = x(0)\,e^{at} \]
Separable first order
\[ \frac{dx}{dt} = f(t)\,g(x) \]
First order linear
\[ \frac{dx}{dt} + P(t)\,x = Q(t) \]
Driven mass-spring-dashpot
\[ mx'' + cx' + kx = F(t) \]
Constant-coefficient homogeneous \(n\times n\) system
\[ \vec{x}' = A\vec{x}, \quad A\text{ a constant }n\times n\text{ matrix} \]
What is a PDE?
Partial differential equation (PDE): An equation involving an unknown function and its partial derivatives. The solution is a function of several variables.

We will encounter problems where the dependence is on space and time, on multiple spatial variables, or both. In Math 412 we focus primarily on the case of two independent variables.

Subscript notation for partial derivatives:

\[ u_t = \frac{\partial u}{\partial t}, \qquad u_{tt} = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2}, \qquad u_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} \]
Examples of PDEs in Math 412
NameEquationType
Simple transport \(u_t + c\,u_x = 0\) Hyperbolic
Wave equation \(u_{tt} = c^2\,u_{xx}, \quad c>0\) Hyperbolic
Heat / diffusion equation \(u_t = k\,u_{xx}\) Parabolic
Laplace's equation \(u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0\) Elliptic
Burgers' equation \(u_t + u\,u_x = 0\) Nonlinear hyperbolic

Laplace's equation describes steady-state / equilibrium solutions of the 2-D heat equation. Burgers' equation is a "toy model" for gas dynamics and the simplest nonlinear wave equation (its solutions develop shock waves).

The first four examples above are prototypical constant-coefficient homogeneous PDEs.

Ch 1.1 – Deriving the Heat Equation

We use the three-step philosophy of applied mathematics:

Formulation – set up a mathematical model
Solution – solve the resulting equations
Interpretation – translate back to the physical problem

Goal

Write equations governing thermal energy transfer in a one-dimensional solid rod via conduction.

Conduction: Transfer of heat due to molecular collisions. Kinetic vibrational energy passes between neighboring molecules even when the molecules themselves move little.
Convection: Moving molecules carry thermal energy from one place to another.

We focus on solids, where conduction is the only mechanism. (Conduction dominates in liquids/gases too when fluid velocity is sufficiently small.)

Physical Quantities

Consider a rod of constant cross-sectional area \(A\) occupying \(0 \le x \le L\). Assume all thermal quantities are uniform across any cross-section.

SymbolMeaningUnits (SI)Known?
\(e(x,t)\) Thermal energy density (per unit volume) J/m³ Unknown
\(\phi(x,t)\) Heat flux: energy per unit time flowing rightward per unit surface area. (\(\phi>0\): flow right; \(\phi<0\): flow left) J/(s·m²) Unknown
\(Q(x,t)\) Sources: energy per unit volume generated per unit time (e.g. chemical reactions, electrical heating) J/(s·m³) Known
Conservation of Heat Energy

Consider an arbitrary segment \(a \le x \le b\). The rate of change of total thermal energy equals the net inflow across boundaries plus internal generation:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\int_a^b A\,e(x,t)\,dx = A\phi(a,t) - A\phi(b,t) + \int_a^b A\,Q(x,t)\,dx. \]

Dividing by \(A\):

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\int_a^b e\,dx = \phi(a,t) - \phi(b,t) + \int_a^b Q\,dx. \]

Deriving the PDE

Since \(a,b\) are constants and \(e\) is differentiable, we may pass the derivative inside the integral. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus gives:

\[ \phi(a,t) - \phi(b,t) = -\int_a^b \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x}\,dx. \]

Combining:

\[ \int_a^b \!\left(\frac{\partial e}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} - Q\right)dx = 0. \]
Key Idea: If the integrand is continuous and this holds for every interval \([a,b]\), then the integrand must be identically zero.

Therefore we obtain the differential equation (conservation law):

\[ \boxed{\frac{\partial e}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} + Q.} \]

One equation, two unknowns (\(e\) and \(\phi\)) — we need more physics.

Temperature, Specific Heat & Fourier's Law

Relating energy density to temperature

SymbolMeaningUnits
\(u(x,t)\)Temperature°C
\(c(x)\)Specific heat (energy per unit mass per unit temperature rise)J/(kg·°C)
\(\rho(x)\)Mass density (mass per unit volume)kg/m³

The thermal energy density is:

\[ e(x,t) = c(x)\,\rho(x)\,u(x,t). \]

Substituting into the conservation law:

\[ c(x)\,\rho(x)\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = -\frac{\partial\phi}{\partial x} + Q. \]

Fourier's Law of Heat Conduction

Fourier's Law (Fourier, 1768–1830): \[ \phi = -K_0\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}, \] where \(K_0\) is the thermal conductivity — a material-specific constant measuring how readily the material conducts heat.

Heat flows from hotter to cooler regions (\(\phi > 0\) when \(\partial u/\partial x < 0\), i.e., temperature decreasing to the right). For a non-uniform rod, \(K_0 = K_0(x)\); for a uniform rod, \(K_0 = \text{const}\).

The Heat Equation

Substituting Fourier's law into the conservation law for a rod with possibly varying material properties:

\[ c(x)\,\rho(x)\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\!\left(K_0(x)\,\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}\right) + Q(x,t). \]

For a uniform rod (constant \(c,\rho,K_0\)) with no sources (\(Q=0\)):

The Heat Equation
\[ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \qquad k = \frac{K_0}{c\rho} > 0 \text{ (thermal diffusivity).} \]

This equation is also called the diffusion equation and is related to Fick's Law in chemistry.

Initial & Boundary Conditions

Why additional conditions are needed

The heat PDE alone has infinitely many solutions. To make unique predictions we need both an initial condition (IC) and boundary conditions (BCs).

Initial condition

Specify the entire temperature profile at \(t=0\):

\[ u(x,0) = f(x), \quad 0 \le x \le L. \]

Boundary conditions at each end

At each endpoint (\(x=0\) or \(x=L\)) choose one of:

  • Prescribed temperature: \(u(\cdot,t) = u_B(t)\)
  • Prescribed heat flux: \(-K_0\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(\cdot,t) = \phi(t)\);   \(\phi\equiv 0\) is "perfectly insulated"
  • Newton's law of cooling: \(-K_0\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(\cdot,t) = h\bigl[u(\cdot,t)-u_B(t)\bigr]\)

The IBVP (zero-temperature ends)

Initial-Boundary Value Problem (IBVP)
\[ \begin{cases} \text{PDE:} & \displaystyle\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}, \quad 0 < x < L,\; t > 0,\\[6pt] \text{BC:} & u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0, \quad t > 0,\\[4pt] \text{IC:} & u(x,0) = f(x), \quad 0 \le x \le L. \end{cases} \]

Goal: find \(u(x,t)\) for \(0 < x < L\), \(t > 0\).

Linear Operators & Homogeneity
Linear operator: An operator \(\mathcal{L}\) satisfying \[ \mathcal{L}(c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2) = c_1\,\mathcal{L}(u_1) + c_2\,\mathcal{L}(u_2) \] for all functions \(u_1,u_2\) and constants \(c_1,c_2\).

The heat operator is:

\[ \mathcal{L}(u) := \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} - k\,\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}. \]

Linearity is verified by direct computation using the linearity of partial derivatives.

A linear homogeneous equation is \(\mathcal{L}(u)=0\); a linear non-homogeneous equation is \(\mathcal{L}(u)=f\) with \(f\ne 0\). The trivial solution \(u\equiv 0\) always solves every linear homogeneous equation.

Homogeneous BCs

A boundary condition is homogeneous when \(u\equiv 0\) satisfies it. For example:

  • Zero temperature ends: \(u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0\) ✓
  • Insulated ends: \(\dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(0,t) = \dfrac{\partial u}{\partial x}(L,t) = 0\) ✓
Principle of Superposition
Principle of Superposition
If \(u_1\) and \(u_2\) each satisfy a linear homogeneous equation (and homogeneous BCs), then so does any linear combination \(c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2\).

The solution space of a linear homogeneous equation forms a vector space. In ODE courses this was finite-dimensional (related to matrix null spaces). For PDEs, the solution spaces are infinite-dimensional — the realm of Fourier series and functional analysis.

Ch 2 – Method of Separation of Variables
Terminology: "Separation of variables" for PDEs is not the same as "separable ODEs." It is an ansatz (educated guess) about the form of the solution, not a property of the equation.

Separation of variables for PDEs is suitable when the PDE and BCs are linear and homogeneous. We seek a product solution:

\[ u(x,t) = \phi(x)\,G(t). \]

First proposed by Daniel Bernoulli in the 1700s.

Applying the ansatz to the heat equation

Substituting into \(u_t = k\,u_{xx}\):

\[ \phi(x)\,G'(t) = k\,\phi''(x)\,G(t). \]

Dividing both sides by \(k\,\phi(x)\,G(t)\):

\[ \frac{1}{kG(t)}\,G'(t) = \frac{1}{\phi(x)}\,\phi''(x). \]

The left side depends only on \(t\); the right side depends only on \(x\). Since \(x\) and \(t\) are independent, both sides must equal the same constant. Writing the constant as \(-\lambda\):

\[ \frac{G'(t)}{kG(t)} = \frac{\phi''(x)}{\phi(x)} = -\lambda. \]

The single PDE separates into two coupled constant-coefficient homogeneous ODEs:

\[ \phi''(x) = -\lambda\,\phi(x), \qquad G'(t) = -\lambda k\,G(t). \]

Applying the zero-temperature BCs \(u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0\) forces:

\[ \phi(0) = \phi(L) = 0. \]

(The alternative \(G(t)\equiv 0\) gives only the trivial solution.)

Eigenvalue Problem
Eigenvalue Problem
Find all \(\lambda\) and non-trivial \(\phi(x)\) satisfying: \[ \phi''(x) = -\lambda\,\phi(x), \quad \phi(0)=0, \quad \phi(L)=0. \]

An eigenvalue–eigenfunction pair \((\lambda, \phi)\) is analogous to an eigenvalue–eigenvector pair \((\lambda, \vec{v})\) for a matrix \(A\vec{v} = \lambda\vec{v}\).

The characteristic polynomial of the ODE is \(r^2 + \lambda = 0\). Three cases:

Case 1: \(\lambda > 0\)

Roots are \(r = \pm i\sqrt{\lambda}\), giving:

\[ \phi(x) = c_1\cos(\sqrt{\lambda}\,x) + c_2\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}\,x). \]

BC \(\phi(0)=0 \Rightarrow c_1=0\). BC \(\phi(L)=0 \Rightarrow c_2\sin(\sqrt{\lambda}\,L)=0\).

For non-trivial solutions: \(\sqrt{\lambda}\,L = n\pi\) for some positive integer \(n\). Thus:

\[ \lambda_n = \left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^{\!2}, \qquad \phi_n(x) = \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n=1,2,3,\ldots \]
Case 2: \(\lambda = 0\)

Double root \(r=0\), so \(\phi(x) = c_1 + c_2 x\). The BCs force \(c_1=c_2=0\). No nontrivial solutions.

\(\lambda=0\) is NOT an eigenvalue here, but it IS for insulated ends or periodic BCs.

Case 3: \(\lambda < 0\)

Roots \(r = \pm\sqrt{-\lambda}\) are real and non-zero: \[\phi(x) = c_1 e^{\sqrt{-\lambda}\,x} + c_2 e^{-\sqrt{-\lambda}\,x}.\] The BCs yield \(c_1+c_2=0\) and \(c_2(1 - e^{-2L\sqrt{-\lambda}})=0\). Since the exponential factor is never 1 for \(\lambda < 0\), we get \(c_1=c_2=0\). No nontrivial solutions.

Time ODE

For each eigenvalue \(\lambda_n\), the time equation \(G' = -\lambda_n k G\) gives:

\[ G_n(t) = e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2\,t}. \]

If \(\lambda < 0\) the time factor would grow exponentially — physically inconsistent with zero-temperature ends, confirming why only \(\lambda_n > 0\) appear.

Product Solutions & Superposition

Each eigenvalue gives a product (separated) solution:

\[ u_n(x,t) = B_n\,\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2\,t}, \quad n=1,2,3,\ldots \]

These satisfy the PDE and both BCs. The constant \(B_n\) is free.

Warning: A typical solution \(u(x,t)\) is not itself a product solution — it is a linear combination of product solutions.

Example 1

Solve the IBVP with IC \(u(x,0) = 4\sin(3\pi x/L)\).

This is exactly the \(n=3\) eigenfunction, so:

\[ u(x,t) = 4\sin\!\left(\frac{3\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(3\pi/L)^2\,t}. \]

Example 2

Solve with IC \(u(x,0) = 7\sin(8\pi x/L)\):

\[ u(x,t) = 7\sin\!\left(\frac{8\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(8\pi/L)^2\,t}. \]

Example 3 (Superposition)

Solve with IC \(u(x,0) = 4\sin(3\pi x/L) + 7\sin(8\pi x/L)\):

\[ u(x,t) = 4\sin\!\left(\frac{3\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(3\pi/L)^2\,t} + 7\sin\!\left(\frac{8\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(8\pi/L)^2\,t}. \]

General finite superposition

If \(u(x,0) = \displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{M} B_n \sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)\), then:

\[ u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{M} B_n\,\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2\,t}. \]
Fourier Series Theory

What if the initial temperature \(u(x,0) = f(x)\) is not a finite combination of sines?

Fourier Sine Series
For "any" \(f(x)\) on \([0,L]\): \[ f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n\,\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \] where the Fourier sine coefficients are: \[ B_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\,\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx, \quad n \ge 1. \]

Key facts about this representation:

  • The formula for \(B_n\) gives the best approximation of \(f\) as a linear combination of the eigenfunctions.
  • For small \(M\) the partial sum may not be very accurate, but it improves as \(M \to \infty\).
  • Under mild conditions on \(f\), the infinite series converges to \(f(x)\).
General Solution of the IBVP

For the IBVP with zero-temperature BCs and IC \(u(x,0)=f(x)\):

\[ u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n\,\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2\,t}, \] where \(\displaystyle B_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\,\sin\!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx\).

This satisfies the heat equation, the BCs \(u(0,t)=u(L,t)=0\), and the IC \(u(x,0)=f(x)\).  Fourier series theory is what makes this work in general.
